# Autonomous Harness Engineering?

One of the most interesting papers I came across this week was Meta-Harness: End-to-End Optimization of Model Harnesses. It builds on a thesis that is becoming increasingly hard to ignore: agent performance is no longer just about model-level optimization. It is also about the harness — the layer that decides what the model sees, what gets stored, what gets retrieved later, and how the overall loop is orchestrated. For years, most of the focus has gone into the model itself. Now creating an autonomous self learning system based on this concept is an interesting problem worth investing into.
 This paper makes the case that harness optimization may be just as important, and I think it's right.
Codex and Claude Code are probably the clearest examples of this in practice. Both wrap the model with sophisticated harnessesharness attempting to optimize instructions, tools, memory/context management, and safety controls that make the system far more useful than the raw model alone. Labs are investing heavily in post training models to their respective harnesses and often it’sIt’s why you see degraded performance when you take a  model like GPT-5.4 outside of the codex harness, for example, and into envs like Cursor or Kilo Code.
What makes Meta-Harness interesting is that it stops treating the harness as fixed and instead treats it as something that can itself be optimized. The architecture is easiest to understand as two nested loops: an inner harness that runs the task and manages context, memory, and state, and an outer loop that acts as an optimization layer around it. That outer loop gives a coding agent access to prior harness candidates — source code, scores, and execution traces — so it can inspect failures, compare versions, and rewrite the harness itself.
In other words, it is doing search over the harness code with an observability layer over prior attempts. Given harness failures are often long-horizon context problems,: a bad retrieval, storage, or presentation decision early on may only surface much later in the rollout. Rather than relying on scalar rewards or compressed summaries, Meta-Harness lets the agent selectively inspect raw history the way a strong engineer would: check the logs, inspect the traces, compare the code, isolate the failure, then change the scaffolding. In the paper’s setup, a single evaluation can generate up to 10 million tokens of diagnostic information, which is exactly why selective access to raw history matters so much.

This paper is a great start and validates that there is real opportunity in building a harness system that evolves, can observe failure, reason over traces, and continuously improve their own retrieval, memory, and orchestration policies without drifting away from the strengths of the underlying model. The best builders are already recognizing that (ie relayer) and taking advantage of the small window to build a new primitive for software design as a whole.
Other Interesting Releases:
Are SLMS and OSS… back?
Gemma 4 drops and suddenly devs can run near-frontier intelligence locally. Pair that with hyper-efficient models like Liquid AI’s LFM2.5-350M and you start to see the shape of something new: hierarchical, multi-agent systems that can run on your own device. If the rate of innovation holds, perhaps this becomes the blueprint for consumer SaaS. Personal AI stops being a thin interface over a centralized model and becomes something persistent, local, and deeply personalized.

Also interested in how this stacks up against things like Perplexity AI’s agentic workflows. This feels like an early signal of verticalized agents competing on execution quality, not just intelligence.
Matthew Gallagher proving Altman right
In ~2 months, with ~$20K, Matthew Gallagher and his brother reportedly built a company to ~$1.8B in annual sales using a stack of Claude Code, ChatGPT, and Grok. It is one of the clearest validations of something Sam Altman has been saying for a while: billion-dollar companies built by extremely small teams. I think we’re still a little bit away from that being the norm but the slope is obvious. Software is being commoditized along with access to intelligence. The winners in my opinion now are those who act with extreme agency and velocity coupled with how well they orchestrate intelligence.
